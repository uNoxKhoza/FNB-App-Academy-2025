GIRAFFE

The giraffe is the tallest land animal on Earth, known for its exceptionally long neck and legs. Native to various regions of Africa, giraffes are well adapted to savannas and open woodlands where they browse on leaves, particularly from acacia trees. Their long necks, which can be up to six feet in length, allow them to reach foliage that few other herbivores can access. Despite having the same number of neck vertebrae as humans (seven), each vertebra in a giraffe is greatly elongated.

Giraffes live in loose social groups, often called towers, which typically consist of females and their young. Males tend to roam more independently or join temporary bachelor herds. They are generally peaceful animals, but males may engage in "necking" battles, where they swing their necks and heads at each other to establish dominance. These gentle giants are listed as vulnerable or endangered in some regions due to habitat loss, poaching, and human encroachment. Conservation programs are working to protect giraffe habitats and promote breeding initiatives. As iconic symbols of African wildlife, giraffes attract tourists and researchers alike, playing a key role in raising awareness about the importance of preserving biodiversity on the continent.
